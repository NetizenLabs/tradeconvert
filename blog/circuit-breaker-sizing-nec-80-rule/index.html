Circuit Breaker Sizing: The 80% Rule and NEC §210.20

Overloading a circuit is a fire hazard; under-sizing a breaker is a nuisance. Mastering the relationship between ampacity and continuous loads is fundamental to NEC compliance.

Under NEC §210.20(A), an overcurrent protection device (circuit breaker) must be sized at no less than 125% of the continuous load plus 100% of the non-continuous load. In simpler terms, for a continuous load, you should never exceed 80% of the breaker's rated ampacity. For a standard 20A breaker, your continuous load limit is 16A.

Continuous vs. Non-Continuous Loads

The first step in proper electrical estimation is identifying the load type. The NEC defines a continuous load as any load where the maximum current is expected to continue for three hours or more (e.g., store lighting, office HVAC). Non-continuous loads are short-duration items like a toaster or a garage door opener. Why the 125% Multiplier?

Standard circuit breakers are thermal-magnetic devices. They generate heat as current passes through them. If a breaker is run at 100% of its capacity for several hours, the heat buildup can cause it to "nuisance trip" even if there isn't a short circuit. The 125% headroom (or the inverse 80% rule) ensures that the breaker stays cool enough to operate within its calibrated trip-curve parameters.

Conductor Sizing and Temperature Terminations

It is not enough to simply size the breaker. Under NEC §310.15, the wire (conductor) must also be capable of carrying the load. A common mistake is using a 20A breaker with 14 AWG wire. This violates the "small conductor rule" in NEC §240.4(D). Always ensure your wire gauge matches the overcurrent protection to prevent the insulation from melting before the breaker can trip.

NEMA Plug Configurations

For industrial and workshop equipment, the NEMA plug type will often dictate the circuit requirements. A NEMA 6-50 plug (common on welders) implies a 50A circuit. If you are unsure of the power requirements for a piece of machinery, check the nameplate for the "Minimum Circuit Ampacity" (MCA) and "Maximum Overcurrent Protective Device" (MOPD) ratings.

Code Reference

NEC §210.20(A): "Where a branch circuit supplies continuous loads or any combination of continuous and noncontinuous loads, the rating of the overcurrent device shall not be less than the noncontinuous load plus 125 percent of the continuous load."

Field Note In commercial settings, some breakers are rated for "100% continuous use." These are specialized, more expensive devices and do not require the 125% multiplier.
