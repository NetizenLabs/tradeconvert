Circuit Breaker Sizing Guide 2026
NEC 80% Rule, Continuous Loads & Calculator Reference

Sizing an overcurrent protection device correctly guarantees electrical safety. Learn standard NEC definitions, thermal thresholds, and wire gauge constraints.

Under NEC §210.20(A), your circuit breaker rating must be at least **125% of the continuous load** plus 100% of the non-continuous load. In practice, this is known as the **80% rule**: your continuous load must never exceed 80% of the breaker's nominal ampacity rating. A standard 20 Amp breaker handles a maximum continuous load of exactly **16 Amps**.

1. Continuous vs. Non-Continuous Loads

Correct load profiling is the first step in electrical design. Under the National Electrical Code (NEC), loads are split into two categories depending on operational duration:

  • Continuous Load: Any load where the maximum current is expected to continue for **3 hours or more** (e.g. store or warehouse lighting, central HVAC systems, office computers).
  • Non-Continuous Load: Short-duration items that cycle on and off quickly (e.g. kitchen disposals, garage door openers, household toasters, microwave ovens).

2. Why the 125% Multiplier is Crucial

Standard molded-case circuit breakers (OCPDs) are thermal-magnetic devices. Current flowing through the breaker generates local heat inside the panel board. If run at 100% rated capacity for hours, the cumulative heat triggers the internal bi-metallic strip to bend, causing a **nuisance trip** even if there is no physical overload or short circuit. The 125% multiplier (or the inverse 80% ceiling) ensures that thermal equilibrium is maintained, protecting the breaker components from premature degradation.

3. Breaker and Conductor Size Reference

It is not enough to size the breaker; the conductor (wire) must also be sized to carry the current. Pairing a 20A breaker with thin 14 AWG wire violates the **Small Conductor Rule** in **NEC §240.4(D)**. Review the standard residential pairings below:

4. NEMA Configurations and Workshop Equipment

In workshop settings, machinery and tool plugs follow strict National Electrical Manufacturers Association (NEMA) limits. A welder with a NEMA 6-50 plug implies a 50A breaker circuit. If you are wiring custom industrial tools, verify the nameplate on the equipment for these two values:

  • Minimum Circuit Ampacity (MCA): Use this value to size your conductor wire.
  • Maximum Overcurrent Protective Device (MOPD): Use this value to size your breaker.

5. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Can I use a 100% rated circuit breaker?

A: Standard residential and commercial breakers are only 80% rated. Commercial 100% rated breakers are specialized, expensive electronic-trip devices requiring specific ventilation and panel enclosures. They do not require the 125% continuous load multiplier.

Q: How do temperature ratings affect wire sizing?

A: Under **NEC Table 310.16**, wires are rated at 60°C, 75°C, or 90°C. Most equipment terminals are limited to 75°C. Even if using a 90°C rated wire, you must calculate ampacity based on the 75°C column if the breaker terminals are rated for 75°C.

Code Reference

NEC §210.20(A): "Where a branch circuit supplies continuous loads or any combination of continuous and noncontinuous loads, the rating of the overcurrent device shall not be less than the noncontinuous load plus 125 percent of the continuous load."

Field Note In commercial settings, some breakers are rated for "100% continuous use." These are specialized, more expensive devices and do not require the 125% multiplier.
